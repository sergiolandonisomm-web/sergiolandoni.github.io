Patagonian Wine: Cool-Climate Elegance Explained from the Territory

A territory-based sommelier’s perspective on why Neuquén and Río Negro are producing some of the most exciting fresh, precise and food-friendly wines in South America.

By Sergio Landoni, Territory-based Sommelier

Published: January 23, 2026 · Updated: April 7, 2026

Wine does not begin in the glass. It begins in the landscape. In Patagonia, that landscape is not an idea, it is something you experience directly.

I live and work here. I walk these vineyards, I taste these wines where they are made. And that changes the way wine is understood.

Desert light, strong winds, Andean water and cool nights shape wines of freshness, precision and finesse rather than weight or excess.

What “Territory-based Sommelier” means

This is not an academic title or a simple bio label. It is a way of practicing sommellerie: being present in the territory, walking the land, and knowing the producers, chefs, hosts, and the people who live it.

This is not a theoretical approach. It comes from being present in the territory: understanding the wind, the irrigation, the producers, the local cuisine, and the people behind each vineyard.

Key point

The same grape tastes completely different depending on the territory. In Patagonia, desert conditions, latitude and cool nights create precision and lift that stand out in the glass.

What changes when wine is told from its place

The same grape variety expresses something entirely different depending on soil, water, diurnal temperature range, and wind. In Neuquén, this is not just a concept, it is something you feel in the glass — especially when exploring the six distinct wine regions of Neuquén .

Telling the story of wine from its place explains why a wine tastes the way it does, and why it belongs to a specific dish, a certain table, or a particular moment of the year. That coherence between regional cuisine and local wine is what defines a complete experience.

My focus is not to lecture, it is to connect. Technique provides the structure; territory provides the meaning.

The role of the sommelier today

A sommelier is no longer just someone who recommends wine in a restaurant. Today, the role is also to build bridges: between producer and table, wine and gastronomy, tourism and culture.

When that connection is solid, the experience is transformed: the glass becomes clear, the wine becomes memorable, and the place becomes part of the memory. This approach is gaining momentum in Patagonia, where territory is becoming central to how wine is communicated.

This broader perspective can also be seen in the growing visibility of sommeliers in Patagonia, where the territory is increasingly taking a central role in the way wine is interpreted and shared.

Neuquén and Río Negro: two faces of Patagonian wine

Neuquén is not a promise — it is already happening. Its viticulture is modern, precise and designed around the conditions of Patagonia: wind, light, and controlled irrigation. The wines are precise, consistent and increasingly relevant in international markets.

Río Negro, in contrast, carries history. In the Alto Valle, old vines of Semillón and Malbec produce wines with natural elegance, moderate structure and a strong sense of identity.

Together, these regions show that Patagonian wine is not defined by a single style. It is defined by place, by how each territory expresses itself through the same varieties.

This diversity is increasingly recognised on the international stage, where Patagonia is understood not as a single region, but as a landscape of distinct and evolving wine territories.

In competitions such as the International Wine & Spirit Competition (IWSC), where judges increasingly look for authenticity, typicity and regional character, Patagonian wines stand out for their freshness, precision and sense of place.

This is not about copying Burgundy or New Zealand. It is about revealing what the Patagonian territory naturally allows: elegance without heaviness, acidity that feels alive, and wines that become memorable because they carry their origin with clarity.

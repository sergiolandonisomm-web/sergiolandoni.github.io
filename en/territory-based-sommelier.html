Patagonian Wine: Cool-Climate Elegance Explained from the Territory

A territory-based sommelier’s perspective on why Patagonia is producing some of the most exciting fresh, precise and food-friendly wines in South America.

By Sergio Landoni, Territory-based Sommelier

Published: January 23, 2026 · Updated: April 7, 2026

Wine does not begin in the glass. It begins in the landscape.

In Patagonia, that landscape is not an idea, it is something you live and experience every day.

I live and work here. I walk these vineyards and taste these wines where they are made. That changes the way wine is understood.

As a territory-based sommelier based in Patagonia, I have made this shift my practice: reading the landscape first, so every glass tells a deeper and more coherent story.

Desert light, constant wind, Andean water and cool nights shape wines defined by freshness, precision and finesse, rather than weight or excess.

Territory-based Sommelier: a shift in perspective

Traditional sommellerie often starts in the glass, analysing structure, aromas and style, and works backwards to the origin.

A territory-based approach does the reverse. It begins in the place.

Landscape, climate, water, people and way of life come before the first pour.

Wine is not just a product to be described. It is a territory to be understood.

Key idea

A territory-based sommelier reads the origin first, so the wine makes sense in the glass.

What changes when wine is told from its place

The same grape variety expresses something entirely different depending on soil, water, diurnal temperature range and wind. In Neuquén, this is not theory, it is something you feel in the glass — especially when exploring the six distinct wine regions of Neuquén .

This perspective creates clearer, more memorable connections at the table. Patagonian wines, with their freshness and precision, integrate naturally with modern European and British cuisine, from seafood and vegetables to lighter, seasonal dishes.

Wines are no longer remembered only by variety. They are remembered by where they come from.

My role is not to explain wine. It is to make the place understandable through the wine.

Technique organises knowledge. Territory gives it meaning.

The role of the sommelier today

A sommelier is no longer just someone who recommends wine in a restaurant. Today, the role is to build bridges between producer and table, wine and gastronomy, tourism and culture.

When that connection is real, the experience changes. The wine becomes clearer, more memorable, and the place becomes part of what remains.

This perspective is gaining strength in Patagonia, where territory is becoming central to how wine is interpreted and communicated.

Patagonian wine: a mosaic of territories

Patagonia is an extensive wine territory that stretches from La Pampa to the southernmost limits of Argentina.

Today, its most consolidated expressions are found in Río Negro and Neuquén, where historical vineyards and modern viticulture define the current identity of Patagonian wine.

Neuquén is not a promise, it is already a reality. Its viticulture is modern, precise and shaped by Patagonian conditions: wind, light and controlled irrigation. The wines show clarity, consistency and an increasing presence in international markets.

Río Negro, in contrast, carries history. In the Alto Valle, old vines of Semillón and Malbec produce wines with natural elegance, moderate structure and a strong sense of identity.

Chubut represents the southern frontier of Argentine viticulture. In areas such as Sarmiento and Trevelin, vineyards develop under extreme conditions, giving rise to wines with tension, freshness and a distinctive sense of place.

Beyond these regions, new developments are emerging in areas such as Santa Cruz and Tierra del Fuego, expanding the limits of what is possible in Argentine wine.

Patagonia is not a single region. It is a growing mosaic of territories, each one defined by its own climate, landscape and identity.

This diversity is increasingly recognised on the international stage. In competitions such as the International Wine & Spirit Competition (IWSC), where authenticity, typicity and sense of place are key, Patagonian wines stand out for their freshness, precision and clarity of origin.

Patagonia is not trying to copy Burgundy or New Zealand. It is revealing what its own extreme territory naturally delivers: elegance without heaviness, vibrant acidity and wines that remain in memory because they carry their origin with clarity.

My role is to help that voice travel further, from the Patagonian landscape to tables in London, Paris, Berlin and beyond.
